As Iceland is surrounded by ocean, it's hardly surprising that seafood is a popular constituent in Icelandic cuisine. Below, you can take a look at some interesting traditional Icelandic dishes!

Kjötsupa – Traditional Lamb meat soup

This soup is made from the tougher bits of the lamb and a variety of Icelandic herbs and vegetables. Most Icelanders eat this during the colder months.

Svið – Sheep’s head

While not too common nowadays, it is thought that the cheeks of a smoked sheep’s head are some of the tastest meat you can eat!

Hákarl – Fermented Shark

This is shark meat that has been cut up and placed in to boxes for 6-9 weeks in order for it to ferment. After this, it is usually hung up for 3-4 months.

Harðfiskur - Dry Fish with Butter

Harðfiskur, or dry fish, is a food from Iceland’s Viking era. It’s one of the more traditional Icelandic foods you can try. When the fish is laid out to dry, it shrinks down to only 9% of its original size. Harðfiskur is best served with a good scoop of butter.

Rúgbrauð - Dark Rye Bread

Rúgbrauð is a traditional rye bread that Icelanders have been eating for many years. It is traditionally baked in a pot or steamed in special wooden casks which have been buried in the ground near a hot spring. This bread is crustless, dark brown, dense and its taste is quite sweet.
